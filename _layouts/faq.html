The Basics

What is a stem splitter?

A stem splitter is software that uses artificial intelligence to separate a mixed audio track into its individual components — typically vocals, drums, bass, and other instruments — each saved as its own isolated audio file. These individual files are called stems.

Stem splitters are used by music producers, DJs, remixers, and audio engineers to access parts of a song that were never provided as separate tracks.

How does a stem splitter work?

A stem splitter uses machine learning models trained on large datasets of multi-track recordings. The AI analyzes a stereo mix's spectral and temporal characteristics — the frequency content and timing of different sound sources — and learns to distinguish between them.

It then reconstructs each source as a separate audio file. Modern stem splitters use deep learning architectures like Demucs (Meta AI), HTDemucs, and MDX-Net, which were purpose-built for music source separation. The three main steps are:

  1. Analysis — algorithms map the mix's frequency, timing, and harmonic content
  2. Extraction — the AI isolates each sound source by filtering out everything else
  3. Output — individual audio files are generated for each stem

What is the difference between a stem splitter and a vocal remover?

A vocal remover only separates vocals from the rest of the track, giving you two outputs: a vocal track and an instrumental. A stem splitter goes much further, separating a song into multiple individual components — typically 4 or more stems: vocals, drums, bass, and other instruments.

Stem splitters are more powerful and versatile. Vocal removers are a simpler subset of the same technology, better suited for quick karaoke creation or basic vocal isolation.

What stems can a stem splitter separate?

Most AI stem splitters separate a track into four core stems: vocals, drums, bass, and other instruments. Advanced tools can go further:

  • Electric guitar, acoustic guitar
  • Piano and keyboards
  • Strings and orchestral instruments
  • Backing vocals vs. lead vocals
  • Kick, snare, and hi-hat as separate stems (on some tools)

The depth of separation depends on the AI model. HTDemucs 6-stem and fine-tuned MDX-Net variants offer the most detailed breakdowns currently available.

DAW Integration

Does Logic Pro have a built-in stem splitter?

Yes. Logic Pro 11 and later include a built-in stem splitter. To use it, right-click an audio region and select Stem Splitter from the context menu. Logic separates the track and places each stem on a new track automatically.

Logic Pro's stem splitter is powered by Apple's own AI, performs particularly well on drums and bass, and is available on Apple Silicon Macs only. It is widely considered one of the best DAW-integrated options available.

Does Ableton have a stem splitter?

Yes. Ableton Live 12.3 and later includes a built-in stem separation feature. Right-click an audio clip in the arrangement or session view and select the stem separation option. Ableton processes stems in the background using its own AI model.

FL Studio also includes a stem splitter using the HTDemucs model. Both DAW-native options are excellent for producers who want stem separation integrated directly into their workflow.

What is the best stem splitter for drums?

For drum isolation specifically, Logic Pro 11/12 is widely praised for producing tight, clean drum stems. Among online tools, StemSplit.io and Gaudio Studio deliver strong drum separation with low bleed from other instruments.

In the open-source space, UVR5 using HTDemucs 6-stem is frequently cited by producers on Reddit and Gearspace as the best free option for drum isolation. The Acon Digital Remix Drums module is also a solid choice for DAW plugin users.

Quality and AI Models

What AI models do stem splitters use?

The most widely used AI models in stem splitters are:

  • Demucs / HTDemucs — developed by Meta AI (Facebook Research), widely regarded as the most accurate open-source model for vocals and drums
  • MDX-Net — winner of the 2021 Sony Music Demixing Challenge, excellent for instrumental separation
  • VR Architecture models — used in UVR5, good for vocal isolation
  • Perseus — LALAL.AI's proprietary model, optimized for speed and quality
  • GSEP — Gaudio Studio's in-house model, one of the highest-performing commercially

Tools like UVR5 let you switch between models manually. Online tools like StemSplit.io run optimized model variants automatically.

How accurate are AI stem splitters?

Modern AI stem splitters are highly accurate for well-produced commercial recordings — particularly electronic music, pop, and hip-hop where instruments occupy distinct frequency ranges. Quality is measured by Signal-to-Distortion Ratio (SDR).

Common limitations include:

  • Bleed — small amounts of one stem leaking into another (e.g., hi-hat showing up faintly in the vocal stem)
  • Artifacts — digital artifacts on complex or densely arranged mixes
  • Genre limitations — acoustic recordings and orchestral music are harder to separate than electronic music

Best-in-class tools like Gaudio Studio and StemSplit.io produce results that are clean enough for professional production use in most scenarios.

Use Cases and Legal

What can I use a stem splitter for?

Stem splitters have a wide range of practical uses across music production, education, and entertainment:

  • Remixing — access individual elements of a track to create new arrangements
  • Sampling — isolate a drum break, bassline, or melody for a new production
  • Karaoke — remove vocals to create an instrumental backing track
  • Music practice — isolate a specific instrument to learn a part by ear
  • Audio restoration — separate stems to repair clipping or noise on individual elements
  • DJ performance — blend stems from different tracks in real-time
  • Music education — analyze how a song is arranged by listening to its components in isolation

Is it legal to use a stem splitter on copyrighted music?

Using a stem splitter on copyrighted music for personal, non-commercial use — such as practicing an instrument, academic study, or private listening — is generally considered fair use in many jurisdictions.

However, publishing, distributing, or selling stems derived from copyrighted recordings without a license is illegal under copyright law in most countries. Remixes and productions using separated stems shared publicly also require clearance from the original rights holders. Always review the terms of service of the tool you use, and obtain appropriate licenses before releasing any content commercially.
